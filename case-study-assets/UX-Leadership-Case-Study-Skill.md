# UX Leadership Case Study Skill

## Philosophy

A UX case study for a leadership role is not a project diary or a design showcase. It is a strategic argument that answers one question for the hiring panel:

**"Can this person lead design at the level we need?"**

Every section, every sentence, every number must serve that argument. If it doesn't, cut it.

The difference between a portfolio that gets interviews and one that doesn't:

| Craft-level portfolio | Leadership-level portfolio |
|---|---|
| "I designed these screens" | "I defined the design approach for the team" |
| Shows Figma frames | Shows decisions and their rationale |
| Lists deliverables | Quantifies outcomes |
| Describes process | Explains why this process, and what changed |
| "We did user research" | "I shifted the team from assumptions to evidence — here's how" |

---

## Step 0: Before Writing Anything

Answer these six questions. If the answers aren't clear, interview the designer before writing.

1. **What was the actual business problem?** (Not the design brief — the upstream pressure.)
2. **What was broken before you got there?** (Process, team, product, or all three?)
3. **What did YOU specifically lead, own, or decide?** (Distinct from the team's work.)
4. **What would NOT have happened without you?** (The counterfactual test.)
5. **What are the numbers?** (Impact: conversion, time, NPS, velocity, cost, retention.)
6. **What did you learn that changed how you work?** (Maturity signal.)

If you can't answer 4 and 5, the case study isn't ready. Never fabricate metrics. Use approximations ("~40% reduction") where exact data isn't available, and label them as such.

## Section Architecture

### The Canonical Structure for Leadership Portfolios

```
1. HOOK          — One sentence: what changed because of this work
2. CONTEXT       — Company, team, your scope, constraints
3. PROBLEM       — The real problem (business + user), not the design brief
4. YOUR ROLE     — What you owned vs what the team delivered
5. PROCESS       — Key decisions, not every step
6. OUTCOMES      — Measurable impact with business context
7. RETROSPECTIVE — What you'd do differently (optional but powerful)
```

### What to Cut Completely

- Methodology name-drops without outcomes ("We used design thinking...")
- Screen inventories ("We designed 47 screens")
- Generic process diagrams that don't show decisions
- Tool mentions that don't serve the argument (Figma, Miro, etc.)
- Research findings that don't connect to design decisions

## Section-by-Section Guide

### 1. HOOK (The Title + Opening Line)

The hook is the most important part of the case study. It should name the *result*, not the project.

**Formula:** `[What changed] for [whom] by [how much] because of [what decision or approach]`

**Weak hooks:**
- "Redesigning the onboarding flow"
- "UX for a fintech mobile app"
- "Improving the checkout experience"

**Strong hooks:**
- "Collapsed 5 fragmented products into one platform — the biggest overhaul in Western Union's history"
- "Reduced 11 designers' delivery cycle by 40% by introducing design QA rituals"
- "Shifted a 6-year-old enterprise product from assumption-driven to evidence-driven design in 90 days"

The opening sentence should be a bold claim that forces the reader to keep reading.

---

### 2. CONTEXT (60–80 words max)

Cover:
- Company and product type
- Your seniority and scope of ownership
- Team size you led or influenced
- Key constraints (time, regulatory, technical)
- What success looked like for the business at the time

Do NOT cover:
- Company background the reader can Google
- The full org structure
- Your personal career path

**Template:**
> *At [Company], I [led/managed/owned] [scope] — a [team size], [product type] operating under [key constraint]. The business needed [outcome]. My mandate was [specific responsibility].*

---

### 3. PROBLEM (The Real Problem)

Most designers describe the design problem. Leadership case studies describe the *upstream* problem — why this project existed, what failure mode it was responding to.

**Structure:**
- **Business tension** — What business outcome was at risk?
- **User reality** — What was the actual user experience failing to deliver?
- **Root cause** — What was broken at the system level (not just the UI)?
- **Stakes** — What happened if nothing changed?

**Leadership signal:** Show that you understood the problem before you touched a design tool.

**Example framing:**
> *The product had 5 separate apps serving the same user with duplicated journeys, inconsistent trust signals, and a cost structure that made global expansion impossible. The UX problem was real but secondary — the primary problem was organizational and architectural.*

---

### 4. YOUR ROLE (Be Precise, Not Humble)

This is where most leadership case studies fail. Designers either undersell ("I was part of a team that...") or overclaim ("I designed the entire product"). Neither builds trust.

**What to include:**
- Your decision rights (what only YOU could approve, block, or redirect)
- Who you managed or mentored
- The cross-functional relationships you held
- The escalations you navigated
- What the team delivered *because of* your direction

**Format: Two columns**
```
What I owned                    | What the team delivered
────────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────
Design strategy & direction     | UI production across 4 squads
Stakeholder alignment           | Prototypes and handoff specs
Hiring and mentorship           | Component library
Scope negotiation with PM & Eng | Research synthesis
```

---

### 5. PROCESS (Decisions, Not Steps)

Do not describe every phase of your process. Describe the 3–5 **decisions** that shaped the outcome.

**Decision frame:**
- **The situation** — What was ambiguous or contested?
- **The options** — What were the real alternatives?
- **What you chose** — The actual decision
- **Why** — The reasoning (user need + business logic + constraint)
- **What happened** — The downstream effect

**Example:**
> *Six weeks into the project, engineering flagged that building a new design system in parallel with production screens was unsustainable. The options were: (1) delay the design system, (2) delay production, or (3) reduce scope. I pushed back on all three and made the case to leadership that a half-built design system would cost more in rework than the delay. We got the additional 3 weeks. The design system shipped before production — and it's the reason 142 screens shipped at consistent quality.*

**Visuals that work in this section:**
- Simplified journey maps showing before/after decision points
- IA diagrams with rationale annotations
- Process timelines that show where you intervened
- "We chose X over Y because..." comparison tables

> **Design Manager rule:** For leadership roles, process artefacts outperform screen showcases. Replace sections titled "Payments Hub" or "Transfer Flow" with sections titled "Design System", "Design Style", and "UX Review Ritual". Screens demonstrate craft. Systems, style decisions, and rituals demonstrate leadership. Hiring panels for Design Manager roles are not evaluating whether you can design a screen — they are evaluating whether you can build a practice that makes *other people's* screens consistently excellent.

### What to show instead of UI screens (for Design Manager portfolios)

| Instead of showing | Show this instead |
|---|---|
| Final UI screens | Design system components and the case made to build them |
| Figma prototypes | Design direction options and how the decision was reached |
| Before/after UI | The review ritual that enforced quality before shipping |
| Screen inventory | A metric: how many screens shipped at consistent quality because of your system |
| Flow diagram | The process artefact (service blueprint, epic map) that aligned 40+ engineers |

**The test:** If you could swap your screens for a competitor's screens and the case study still reads correctly — the screens aren't doing the work. Replace them with the decisions and systems only you built.

---

### 6. OUTCOMES (Metrics + Business Context)

Numbers without context are noise. Context without numbers is opinion.

**Structure:**
```
Metric           | Before  | After   | Business meaning
─────────────────┼─────────┼─────────┼────────────────────────────────
Drop-off rate    | ~22%    | ~17%    | ~5% improvement across key journeys
Design rework    | ~40hrs  | ~24hrs  | 40% reduction, faster engineering delivery
Handoff cycle    | 3 days  | 2.4 days| ~20% time saved per sprint
```

**What makes a metric credible at leadership level:**
- Connect to a business KPI (revenue, retention, cost, speed)
- Be honest about approximations ("~" prefix where exact data isn't available)
- Name the measurement method if non-obvious
- Mention what you would measure next (shows ownership beyond launch)

**Outcomes to always look for:**
- Delivery velocity change (sprints, handoff time, rework cycles)
- Product quality change (error rates, support tickets, user testing scores)
- Team performance change (attrition, output, onboarding time)
- Business metric change (conversion, NPS, revenue, cost)
- Org/process change (rituals adopted, practices scaled)

---

### 7. RETROSPECTIVE (Optional, High Signal)

The most effective way to signal leadership maturity. A designer who can clearly articulate what they'd do differently — and why — is far more credible than one who presents a perfect project.

**What to include:**
- One thing you'd change about your approach
- One thing you underestimated
- One thing that surprised you about the org/team/user

**What NOT to do:**
- Fake humility ("I'd communicate even more next time")
- Re-open wounds ("The stakeholders were really difficult")
- Vague lessons ("I learned the importance of research")

**Strong retrospective example:**
> *I'd build the research ops infrastructure earlier. We ran good research but it lived in Confluence pages that nobody read. By month 6, designers were duplicating research that already existed. I'd invest in a shared synthesis layer from day one.*

## Writing Style Guide for Leadership Case Studies

### Voice

- First person, active voice: "I led", "I decided", "I pushed back"
- Specific over general: "11 designers across 3 squads" not "a large team"
- Confident, not boastful: state facts, let the reader conclude "impressive"
- No design jargon without payoff: don't say "design thinking" unless you're going to explain what specifically you did

### Length Per Section

| Section | Target length |
|---|---|
| Hook/Title | 1 sentence (10–15 words) |
| Context | 60–80 words |
| Problem | 80–120 words |
| My Role | 2-column table + 1 paragraph |
| Process | 3–5 decisions, 60–100 words each |
| Outcomes | Table + 1 paragraph interpretation |
| Retrospective | 60–80 words |
| **Total** | **400–700 words** |

A case study for a leadership portfolio should be readable in 4–6 minutes. If it's longer, it needs editing, not more content.

### The One-Screen Test

Print the case study at A4. If the key argument — what you did, what resulted, what you'd do differently — is not clear on first glance, it's not tight enough.

## What Signals Leadership to a Hiring Panel

| Signal | How it appears in the writing |
|---|---|
| Strategic framing | Problem described in business terms first, UX terms second |
| Decision ownership | Uses "I decided" and "I pushed back" not "we explored" |
| Tradeoff fluency | Explicitly names what was sacrificed and why |
| Stakeholder awareness | Names who had to be convinced and how |
| Team multiplier | Makes the team's success visible, not just their own output |
| Business literacy | Connects design choices to revenue, cost, or risk |
| Learning posture | Retrospective is honest and specific |
| Scope ownership | Clear about what they owned vs what they contributed to |

## QA Checklist Before Publishing

**Content:**
- [ ] The hook names a result, not a project type
- [ ] The problem describes a business tension, not a design brief
- [ ] "I" appears in sections describing leadership acts
- [ ] Every metric has business context
- [ ] No section is longer than necessary
- [ ] Jargon is either explained or removed
- [ ] A retrospective exists (or a deliberate reason it doesn't)

**Credibility:**
- [ ] All metrics are either verified or labelled as approximate
- [ ] Nothing claims sole ownership of team output
- [ ] The counterfactual is clear: what wouldn't have happened without you
- [ ] The role section distinguishes ownership from contribution

**Format:**
- [ ] Readable in under 6 minutes
- [ ] No wall-of-text paragraphs (max 4 lines per paragraph)
- [ ] Visuals used only where they communicate faster than words
- [ ] Titles are outcome statements, not section labels

## Common Failure Modes in Leadership Case Studies

| Failure mode | What it signals to reviewers | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "We designed..." throughout | Can't distinguish own leadership from team output | Rewrite with specific ownership |
| No metrics | Can't connect design to business | Find proxies; use "~" honestly |
| Perfect story | Lacks intellectual honesty | Add retrospective |
| Too long | Can't edit; doesn't respect reader's time | Cut to 500 words |
| Process as hero | Confuses method with impact | Lead with outcome, use process to support |
| Jargon-heavy | Hiding behind vocabulary | Plain language test: explain to a PM |
| Generic role description | Didn't differentiate | Use the 2-column ownership table |
