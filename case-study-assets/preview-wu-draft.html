The Problem

WU ran five separate apps for the same user. Each had its own stack, team, and experience. The business model shift to account-based required one platform — not five.

My Role
Design Strategy & Direction
Set the end-to-end UX vision for E1, translating business objectives into a coherent design direction across all platforms and markets.
Investment Case for Nexus Design System
Made the case to CPO and Engineering leadership for a dedicated design system effort before a single production screen was designed.
Dual-Track Agile Process Design
Introduced and embedded dual-track agile across squads — keeping discovery one sprint ahead of delivery to prevent engineering bottlenecks.
Epic Sequencing & RICE Prioritisation
Collaborated with Product to build the delivery roadmap using RICE, sequencing design dependencies as first-class constraints.
Stakeholder Alignment
Held alignment across CPO, Engineering VPs, Compliance, and Brand — negotiating scope trade-offs while protecting core UX outcomes.
Design QA Ritual Design & Enforcement
Created and enforced a pre-launch UX review ritual — a quality gate between design approval and engineering shipment that reduced rework by 40%.
Team Leadership — 11 Designers
Led weekly 1:1s, critique sessions, and career development conversations across a multi-disciplinary team embedded in four agile squads.
Key Decisions
Decision 01
Design system before production screens
Six weeks in, engineering flagged that building a design system in parallel with production screens was unsustainable. I pushed back on delaying, reducing, or dropping the system — made the case to the CPO that a half-built system would cost more in rework than a short delay. We got three additional weeks. The Nexus Design System shipped before production. That decision is why 142 screens shipped at consistent quality.

Design System

Before a single production screen was designed, I made the case to leadership for a dedicated design system effort. The argument: without a shared foundation, 142 screens across 5 platforms would ship at 142 different quality levels. The Nexus Design System was the insurance policy for scale.

The case made
I framed the system not as a design need but as an engineering cost reduction — every inconsistent component meant a rework cycle. Leadership approved a 3-week investment before production began. It was the highest-leverage decision of the entire project.

Foundation beyond E1

The Nexus Design System — a shared foundation of components, tokens, and standards that unified the visual language across iOS, Android, and Web.

Design Style

Four design directions were explored and presented to leadership, each resolving the same core screen with a different visual language and rationale. Direction 01 received the most votes and aligned with the core aesthetics of Western Union — it went through a second round of critique before becoming the foundation of E1’s visual language.

Selected Direction — Direction 01
Modern & account-based High contrast Warm brand heritage WCAG 2.1 AA compliant Global low-literacy market ready

Balanced WU’s brand warmth with a modern, fintech-grade product language. High contrast ratios met accessibility compliance across all markets. Clear visual hierarchy reduced cognitive load for low-literacy users. Enough visual elevation to compete with challenger fintech products without abandoning the trust signals WU users relied on.

Direction 1 — Scannable, minimalistic, clear and concise with a subtle burst of colour. Selected by leadership as the foundation for E1’s visual language.

UX Review Ritual

Before launch, I introduced a structured UX evaluation process — a quality gate that sat between “design approved” and “engineering shipped”. This was not a visual QA pass. It was a systematic review of the complete application against the original experience intent.

What it covered
👤
Usability
Can users complete the task without error or confusion?
▶
Accessibility
Does it meet WCAG 2.1 AA across all primary flows?
■
Consistency
Does it match the Nexus Design System in every component?
🛡
Trust
Does the UI earn user confidence at each step of the flow?
Why it worked
Most design QA happens informally and too late. By formalising it as a ritual with a structured template and a named owner, it became predictable — engineers knew when it was coming, what it covered, and how findings would be actioned. Rework dropped by 40% within two sprints of adoption.
What it became
The ritual outlasted the project. It was adopted by squads not on E1 as a standard practice. It’s now part of how Western Union’s design team ships globally.

Hot-Fix Cadence
Identify and prioritise issues from analytics weekly. A dedicated squad of designers works exclusively on hot-fix optimisations, keeping the shipped experience continuously improving post-launch.

Once optimisations are in rhythm, the remaining design team moves on to adapting and extending the E1 experience for new markets and regions — building on the Nexus Design System as the shared foundation.
