The Problem
Western Union had 5+ disconnected apps with duplicate efforts, legacy debt, and no unified customer model

R3, R4, US Wallet, EU Banking App — each market built and maintained separately, creating duplicated engineering effort, inconsistent user experiences, and no path to scale. Customers couldn't build a relationship with WU because every touchpoint felt like a different product.

My Role & Contribution
Design Manager — led 11 designers across the biggest redesign in WU's history

Full end-to-end ownership from 2-day strategy workshop through UX QA and launch. Single design authority across a cross-functional team of 40+ engineers. Balanced global brand consistency, Canada-specific localisation (EN/FR), and KYC/AML compliance across every screen.

Three Key Design Decisions
1
Account-based model over transaction model: Shifted WU's fundamental product paradigm from "send a transfer" to "manage your financial life." This is what made loyalty, wallets, and cross-sell structurally possible — a transaction model cannot support them.
2
Design system as prerequisite, not a parallel track: Insisted on building Nexus DS before designing production screens. This was the non-negotiable decision that made 142 screens across 7 payment methods ship at consistent quality — without it, the redesign would have fragmented under its own scale.
3
Dual-track agile with embedded designers: Championed running discovery ahead of delivery so engineers always had validated, build-ready designs. Embedded designers directly into agile squads — eliminating the handoff bottleneck that had caused delays in every previous WU project.

01 — The Challenge

Seven Forces Working Against Us

Redesigning the entire Western Union digital product from the ground up — while the business kept running — meant navigating seven simultaneous constraints.

01
Legacy Systems & Technical Debt

Getting buy-in from senior leadership to challenge the technical limitations that had been accepted as fixed constraints for years. The design had to prove a better experience was worth the engineering investment to unlock it.

02
Cross-Functional Alignment

Product, Engineering, and Business teams had never worked this tightly together. Being embedded in grooming sessions as the design voice — not just a delivery resource — was new territory that required earning trust at every step.

03
Resource Constraints

Prioritising work with a lean team at the pace the delivery required — and decisively guiding junior designers to stay on track — without sacrificing quality or design intent in the process.

04
Limited Access to Real Users

Partnered with local research agencies and remote usability testing tools (UserTesting) to build evidence-based design decisions despite the access constraints that enterprise compliance environments create.

05
Design–Engineering Collaboration Gaps

Hosted daily/weekly design–engineering syncs to align on feasibility and timelines. Created interactive prototypes to eliminate ambiguity around animations, transitions, and edge-case interactions.

06
Internal Feedback Overload

Structured feedback rounds with a designated final decision-maker for each phase. Without this, the volume of stakeholder opinions would have created an endless revision cycle that no deadline could survive.

02 — Objectives

Strategic Imperatives & Operational Goals

The redesign served two sets of objectives simultaneously — long-term strategic ambition and near-term operational efficiency.

Strategic Imperatives
  • Engage receivers and elevate their status as core drivers of revenue and user acquisition — breaking WU's sender-only model
  • Expand the product suite beyond money transfer — loyalty, digital wallets, and financial services built into the platform
  • Move to an account-based model: users manage financial life, not just individual transactions
  • Deliver an integrated omni-channel experience that connects digital and retail through a unified wallet
  • Modernise front-end platforms to improve customer experience through better company alignment and faster global delivery
Operational Drivers
  • Eliminate duplication across R3 (web/mobile), R4 (web/mobile), US Wallet, and EU Banking App — one platform replaces all
  • Unify the technology stack, streamline teams, and increase organisational alignment to lower cost and boost speed
  • Trim licensing, maintenance, and infrastructure deployment costs through consolidation
  • Simplify architecture through decoupling of services and creating modular, reusable components and systems

04 — Process & Roadmap

From Strategy Workshop to Launch

A deliberate, phased process across 12 steps — ensuring discovery led delivery and design decisions were validated before any engineering investment was committed.

01
2-Day Product Strategy Workshop

Brought together Product, Business, and Engineering leadership to align on vision, constraints, and priorities before any design work began. Set the shared understanding that made cross-functional trust possible throughout the project.

02
Competitor IA Analysis

Deep analysis of information architectures across Wise, Remitly, and the existing WU R4 app. Identified what competitors were doing well, what gaps existed, and where WU had an opportunity to differentiate through structure and hierarchy.

03
Card Sorting & IA Validation

Created multiple IA options and tested them with a limited audience. Card sorting revealed the user's mental model of how financial features should be organised — the results informed the master IA that became the structural foundation of E1.

04
Master User Flow & Service Blueprint

Mapped the complete sender journey end-to-end in a service blueprint — covering touchpoints, systems, backend dependencies, and failure states. This became the single source of truth for engineering planning and design prioritisation.

05
Epic Prioritisation (RICE Framework)

In collaboration with Product, used RICE (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort) to create a clear roadmap of feature delivery. Each Epic spanned two sprints, with design dependencies mapped explicitly to prevent engineering bottlenecks.

06
Nexus Design System

Built a robust, scalable design system to serve well into the future — making the case for dedicated resources to unite all WU digital assets under one roof. This was the non-negotiable prerequisite that made consistent quality possible at scale.

07
UX Jam Sessions

Cross-functional ideation sessions that brought designers, engineers, and product managers together to rapidly explore solutions before committing to a direction. Built shared ownership of the design decisions across the team.

08
Wireframes & Master Flows

Created master flows and screen wires in parallel with design system component development — ensuring every screen was buildable from components that already existed or were in progress.

09
4 Design Style Directions

Created four distinct design directions for senior leadership to evaluate and select from. Direction 1 received the most votes and aligned most strongly with the core aesthetics of Western Union — becoming the visual foundation for all production screens.

10
Production Design & Handoff

Full high-fidelity design of 142+ screens across all states, variants, and platforms (iOS, Android, Web). Delivered via Figma Dev Mode with annotated specs and interactive prototypes for complex interactions.

11
UX Review Before Launch

As the most critical quality measure — a comprehensive UX evaluation of the complete application before release. Provided inputs to make the shipped experience as close to the original design intent as possible.

12
Launch & Monitor

Launched with a squad of designers dedicated to weekly hot-fix optimisations based on Amplitude and Quantum Metrics data, user feedback, and behaviour patterns — while a second squad moved forward on adaptation for additional regions.

06 — Design Directions

Four Directions. One Decision.

To align senior leadership on the visual direction without endless iteration, four distinct design styles were created and put to a structured vote.

Direction 01
Modern Premium

Clean, bold typography with the WU yellow used as a high-contrast accent. Influenced by premium fintech brands like Revolut — confident, trustworthy, and clearly digital-native. This direction most strongly aligned with the core aesthetics of Western Union's brand vision.

★ Selected by Leadership
Direction 02
Warm & Human

Softer colour palette with lifestyle photography as a central visual device. Emphasised emotional connection with the diaspora community. Rejected because it risked reducing clarity in the transfer flow.

Direction 03
Functional Clarity

Data-forward, utility-first. Minimal visual decoration, maximum information density. Closely inspired by Wise's design language. Rejected as too clinical — didn't convey the loyalty and engagement dimension of the new platform.

Direction 04
Heritage Evolved

Grounded in WU's 170-year heritage — warmer, more traditional. Tested well with older demographics but scored lowest on modernity. The brand evolution it implied was too conservative for the scale of the redesign ambition.
